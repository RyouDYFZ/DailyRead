## 文章摘要

伦敦大学学院（UCL）一项针对1.9万名英国儿童的纵向研究发现，幼年遭受体罚与GCSE考试成绩不佳及青少年期风险行为增加相关。研究指出，体罚对儿童毫无益处。尽管苏格兰和威尔士已全面禁止体罚未成年人，英格兰和北爱尔兰仍保留‘合理惩戒’抗辩。研究人员和部分政界人士呼吁法律改革，但英国教育部表示暂无修法计划。

## Reading Passage

A comprehensive study conducted by University College London (UCL) has revealed concerning links between the use of physical punishment in early childhood and poorer academic outcomes at the secondary-school level. Tracking a cohort of roughly 19,000 children born in the UK between 2000 and 2002, researchers found that those who were smacked were more likely to record lower GCSE grades and to engage in riskier behaviours during adolescence. The large-scale, longitudinal design allowed the team to follow participants from infancy through to their teenage years, capturing a rich array of behavioural and educational data. The findings arrive at a time when the legality of smacking remains a fractured issue across the UK: while Scotland and Wales have enacted complete bans on physical chastisement of minors, England and Northern Ireland still permit a defence of “reasonable punishment.” This discrepancy has prompted fresh calls from child welfare experts and some policymakers to reconsider the legal framework.

Drawing on detailed records from the National Pupil Database, the investigation homed in on the GCSE performance of 7,559 students. After adjusting for a range of socioeconomic, demographic, and family-level variables—including household income, parental education, and family structure—the data indicated that children who had experienced smacking in their formative years faced a 5.7 percentage point increase in the probability of failing to achieve five or more GCSE passes at grades 9 to 4. This threshold represents the commonly accepted minimum for progressing to further education or training. The researchers defined smacking as the deliberate use of physical force with the intention of causing pain but not injury, typically applied in response to a child’s misbehaviour. Beyond academic attainment, the study’s behavioural analysis painted an equally stark picture. Looking at a sample of 14-year-olds, the researchers found that those who had been subject to physical punishment at the ages of three, five, or seven were 33% more likely to exhibit a constellation of risky behaviours, including involvement in bullying, physical fighting, and other conduct issues. Such patterns held even when controlling for other known risk factors, suggesting an independent association between early physical punishment and later behavioural problems.

Lead researcher Anja Heilmann minced no words when interpreting the results. “Smacking does no good whatsoever,” she asserted. “It does not help children and all the effects that we did find were in the direction of a harmful outcome.” Heilmann acknowledged the study’s observational design precludes definitive causal claims, yet she underscored that the consistency and direction of the adverse associations—across both educational and behavioural measures—lends weight to the argument that physical punishment is not a benign disciplinary tool. The research forms part of a wider evidence review, joining a growing international scientific consensus that questions the effectiveness and safety of smacking as a parenting practice. Similar longitudinal studies in countries such as the United States and Canada have reported comparable patterns, reinforcing the notion that physical discipline may carry long-term developmental costs.

The legal environment surrounding parental discipline diverges sharply across the UK’s nations. Scotland led the way by outlawing the physical punishment of children under 16 in 2020, and Wales enacted a similar prohibition in 2022. In both jurisdictions, the longstanding common-law defence of reasonable chastisement has been abolished, meaning that any act of hitting a child is now illegal, regardless of the parent’s intent. By contrast, England and Northern Ireland have retained this legal defence, despite sustained pressure from campaigners who argue it contravenes children’s rights and runs counter to international conventions on the protection of minors. The Department for Education in England has stated bluntly that there are “no plans to change the law,” a stance that has frustrated child protection groups and medical bodies. In Northern Ireland, however, Justice Minister Naomi Long has signalled an openness to reform, noting her support for removing the defence of reasonable punishment. Her intervention has been welcomed by children’s charities, although any legislative change would require approval from the Northern Ireland Assembly, where opinions on the matter remain divided.

Not all scholars are persuaded that altering the law is the most effective response to the UCL findings. Ellie Lee, a professor at the University of Kent and a representative of the campaign group Be Reasonable England, cautioned against reducing complex developmental outcomes to a single behavioural variable. “Most things in child development are multifactorial,” she observed, implying that smacking rarely operates in isolation from other aspects of family life, such as parental stress, the quality of the overall parent–child relationship, and socioeconomic context. Lee’s reservations echo a broader methodological caution: because the study is observational, it cannot prove that smacking directly causes lower grades or risky behaviour. While the statistical controls strengthen the associations, unmeasured variables or reverse causation cannot be entirely ruled out. For instance, children with more challenging temperaments may elicit harsher parenting, and it may be those pre-existing difficulties—rather than the smacking itself—that contribute to later problems. The research team themselves were careful to stress that the study does not establish a direct causal link.

As the evidence accumulates, the conversation is increasingly framed as one of safeguarding children’s developmental potential. The GCSE is a high-stakes qualification that heavily influences post-secondary options, so any factor that may systematically depress attainment warrants serious policy consideration. Campaigners emphasize that legal reform would not criminalise ordinary parents but would send a clear public-health message that physical punishment carries unacceptable risks. At the same time, legislators must contend with deeply held cultural norms around discipline and the rights of families to raise children without excessive state interference. The Department for Education’s position suggests that, in England at least, the political appetite for change is limited, despite the mounting research. In Northern Ireland, the shifting tone from senior ministers may keep the door open to future legislative evolution. The UCL study thus not only adds empirical detail to a contested subject but also keeps the pressure on lawmakers to weigh whether current legal provisions adequately protect the educational and personal wellbeing of the next generation. Ultimately, the discussion goes beyond smacking to encompass broader questions about how society can best support parents in nurturing children’s learning and development from the earliest years.

## 长难句理解

> After adjusting for a range of socioeconomic, demographic, and family-level variables—including household income, parental education, and family structure—the data indicated that children who had experienced smacking in their formative years faced a 5.7 percentage point increase in the probability of failing to achieve five or more GCSE passes at grades 9 to 4.

**语法要点：**介词短语作状语 + 破折号插入语 + 宾语从句 + 定语从句

**分析：**本句以时间介词短语'After adjusting...'开头，说明在控制了多项变量之后；破折号中间插入列举，补充说明变量的具体内容。主句为'the data indicated that...'，后接宾语从句，其中嵌套一个who引导的定语从句修饰children。

**翻译：**在对一系列社会经济、人口和家庭层面的变量（包括家庭收入、父母受教育程度和家庭结构）进行调整后，数据表明，在成长时期遭受过体罚的儿童未能取得五门或以上GCSE 9至4分及格成绩的概率增加了5.7个百分点。


> Heilmann acknowledged the study’s observational design precludes definitive causal claims, yet she underscored that the consistency and direction of the adverse associations—across both educational and behavioural measures—lends weight to the argument that physical punishment is not a benign disciplinary tool.

**语法要点：**并列结构 + 宾语从句 + 插入语 + 同位语从句

**分析：**该句包含两个并列分句，由'yet'连接。前半句中'acknowledged'后省略了that的宾语从句；后半句中'underscored that...'带有一个长宾语从句，其中破折号插入语补充说明关联性广泛存在于教育和行为指标中，最后又嵌套一个that引导的同位语从句说明'argument'的内容。

**翻译：**海尔曼承认该研究的观察性设计排除了确定的因果推断，但她强调，不良关联在教育和行为指标上表现出的方向一致性为‘体罚并非无害的惩戒工具’这一论点增添了分量。


> While the statistical controls strengthen the associations, unmeasured variables or reverse causation cannot be entirely ruled out.

**语法要点：**让步状语从句

**分析：**本句使用'while'引导让步状语从句，表示‘尽管……但是……’的逻辑关系，主句说明无法完全排除未测量变量或反向因果的可能性。

**翻译：**尽管统计控制加强了这些关联，但无法完全排除未测量变量或反向因果的可能性。


> The UCL study thus not only adds empirical detail to a contested subject but also keeps the pressure on lawmakers to weigh whether current legal provisions adequately protect the educational and personal wellbeing of the next generation.

**语法要点：**not only... but also... 平行结构 + 不定式目的状语 + 宾语从句

**分析：**该句使用'not only... but also...'平行结构，连接两个动词短语；'to weigh whether...'为不定式作目的状语，其中'whether'引导宾语从句。

**翻译：**因此，伦敦大学学院的研究不仅为一个充满争议的话题增添了实证细节，而且也持续向立法者施加压力，促使他们权衡现行法律条款是否充分保护了下一代的教育和个人福祉。

## 习语与地道表达

**mince no words** 直言不讳，说话不拐弯抹角

当某人直截了当地表达批评性观点时使用，语体中性偏非正式。

例句：The critic minced no words when reviewing the poorly researched documentary.


**paint a stark picture** 描绘出一幅严峻的画面；明确揭示令人担忧的情况

常用于新闻报道中，描述清楚且令人警醒的调查结果。

例句：The report paints a stark picture of rising inequality across the region.


**keep the door open** 保持可能性，留有余地

可用于谈判或未来行动未被排除的语境。

例句：The company kept the door open for further talks with the union.


**run counter to** 与……背道而驰，违反

正式用语，常与原则、规则或预期搭配。

例句：Such practices run counter to the organization’s stated values.
